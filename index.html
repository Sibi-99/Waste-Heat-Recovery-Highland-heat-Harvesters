Understanding Waste Heat Recovery

New Here? Start with the Basics

Before jumping into numbers, here's a plain-English guide to what waste heat recovery is, why it matters, and why this platform exists.

Every building is throwing money — and heat — into thin air.

Factories, offices, data centres, and warehouses all consume energy to heat spaces, run processes, and ventilate air. But most of that energy doesn't actually stay in the building — it escapes as warm exhaust air, hot wastewater, or heat through vents and ducts. This is called waste heat, and in most buildings, 25–55% of input energy is simply lost this way.

So how does Waste Heat Recovery actually work?

It's simpler than it sounds. A WHR system acts like a recycling loop for thermal energy that would otherwise be thrown away.

🏭
1

Heat is Generated

Your building or process produces heat as a byproduct — from exhaust air, hot water, industrial ovens, server racks, or boiler flue gases.

🌡️
2

Heat is Captured

A heat exchanger or heat pump intercepts the warm stream before it escapes — extracting as much thermal energy as possible without disrupting operations.

🔄
3

Heat is Redirected

That captured energy is transferred to where it's actually needed — preheating incoming air, warming water, or supplementing space heating systems.

💰
4

Energy Bills Drop

Because you're reusing heat you already paid to generate, you need to buy less gas or electricity — directly cutting costs and carbon emissions.

☕

Think of it like a thermos flask for your building.

When you pour hot coffee into a normal mug, the heat escapes into the room — wasted. A thermos captures that heat and keeps it inside. A waste heat recovery system works the same way. Instead of letting your building's thermal energy escape through vents, ducts, and exhausts, it captures that heat and routes it back to where it's needed. The result? You buy less energy to keep the building warm, processes running, and water hot. Every unit of recovered heat is one less unit you pay your energy supplier for.

💷

It Saves Real Money

For a typical commercial building or industrial facility, installing a WHR system can cut heating-related energy bills by 15–40% every year. Over a typical system lifetime of 15–20 years, this adds up to a significant return on investment.

15–40%
annual energy cost reduction (Ononogbo et al., 2023)
🌿

It Reduces Carbon Emissions

Less energy bought means fewer CO₂ emissions. As the UK grid decarbonises, every kWh saved avoids approximately 0.21 kg of CO₂ — contributing directly to net-zero targets and corporate sustainability commitments under TCFD and SECR.

20–35%
reduction in operational carbon emissions
📈

It Pays for Itself

The capital cost of installing a WHR system is typically recovered through energy savings within 3–7 years, depending on your building size, operating hours, and energy tariff. After that, the savings are pure financial gain.

3–7 yrs
typical simple payback period

So why isn't everyone already using it?

The technology works — the evidence is clear. But research consistently shows that the main barrier to adoption isn't technical or even financial. It's informational. Decision-makers simply don't have an easy way to see what WHR would mean for their specific building.
